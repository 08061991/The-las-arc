### Why chase perfection when you already wrote it?

When you’re an artist, the biggest thing you want is to be remembered. To mark yourself into the annals of history. To be timeless. To that end, The La’s will always be something of a peculiar study in pop music history, because they achieved those aims in spite of their lack of official recordings. Rarely has a band been so influential on the pop scene, left it’s mark on so many in its home city and beyond, and written some timeless classics, but only actually had one album of official output, one, it turns out they didn’t even like.

The stories behind the La’s are well-documented, and beyond expanding on the paradox of Lee Mavers’ meticulous perfection-chasing, I don’t intend to go into them. Much better articles have been written than mine, and the fantastic *[In Search Of The La’s - A Secret Liverpool](https://www.amazon.co.uk/Search-Las-Secret-Liverpool/dp/1900924633)* is a humorous, well-researched, enlightening and at times heart-breaking read on the band’s formation, rise, and demise. Perhaps I’ll come onto it in more detail one day, but today, I’m mostly here to extoll the virtues of their greatest moment, that of 1987/1988/1989/1990/1994’s *There She Goes.* There are a lot of versions, after all.

I probably don’t need to introduce the song to you, because you’ve heard it a thousand times, I’m sure. It’s been used in films, and even infamously in a DFS advert. That moment perhaps is the most egregious, and also the most memorable, I imagine. But forget all of that. The song is a shimmering beauty of perfect guitar chords, dreamy lyrics, and singalong catchiness. It’s perfect.

I mean, just *listen* to it:

I confess my own bias, as it rather puts me in mind of happier times, both real and imagined; the idea of being carefree, young, and maybe somewhat stupid (as the band themselves may have been back then!) seems encapsulated in its melody. It’s joyous and cheerful. It makes you smile. All that from a song that no one really knows the inspiration behind. It has been attached to heroin by some observers, although this has been strongly denied through the years, while others think it’s about a girl, which seems the most obvious interpretation. One thing’s for sure, Mavers can’t tell us - according to an anecdote around the song’s recording, Lee was ‘off his tits’ to use the Scouse parlance on some substance or another, and went to bed convinced he’d used his time in the studio to belt out *Tulips From Amsterdam.* Instead, it turned out he’d written the greatest pop song of all time. Sheer perfection.

The irony of the song’s somewhat ersatz origins is that Mavers spent years, maybe *decades*, chasing perfection, famously calling the album a ‘snake with a broken back’ and referring to the, in his mind, bastardisation of certain songs in blunt terms (“Timeless Melody? You mean Tuneless Malady?” being one of the milder ones). The sheer number of ‘album cuts’, versions, bootlegged songs, things recorded on a cassette tape, etc, etc. is proof of that. Peculiarly, the amount of lore versus official versions actually seems to *hurt* remembrance of the band, who remain primarily known for the song I’ve opined so romantically on throughout this piece.

Indeed, outside of the obvious, it’s one hell of an album, and there’s some truly majestic recordings out there. As I pore over the album now, there’s songs like *Looking Glass,* the seemingly impenetrably autobiographical *Son Of A Gun,* or even the aforementioned *Timeless Melody* that don’t get a look-in amongst anyone bar diehards like myself when the band’s history is recalled.

*Ironically, even I’ve chosen a demo version. Even I don’t know what the best version of some of these songs is.*

Worse yet, some potential classics, like the almost-mythical *Human Race* or *Fishin’ Net/Was It Something I Said*, seemingly will never see the light of day outside of bootleg or live recordings (this [astonishingly good cover](https://youtu.be/r8S0qViazQ4?si=Mc_rRMluY8uIslW-) of the latter by The Kowloons aside) because of Mavers’ drive for the ultimate realisation of a sound only he might be able to describe, much less put down on acetate. Better to destroy something imperfect than let people hear it, it seems.

There’s millions of people who have opined on why, tomes have been written, people have been quoted, but the one person who never revealed the real reasons was the only person who could, Mavers himself. Always obfuscating and opaque, more cryptic than the *Times* crossword, it seems almost by design that we’ll never really know what drove Mavers to write such great music and also to the brink of insanity.

It seems tragic to think that the only La’s stuff put to record is one disowned album, a bunch of b-sides and discarded alternate versions, and one shit-hot compilation of live sessions - seriously, The La’s *BBC In Session* video may be the closest to Mavers’ vision ever put to tape, *There She Goes* aside, ironically, which has a peculiarly electric composition - but it’s even more tragic to think that he was chasing perfection for so many years *when he’d already written it,* and in the most simplistic of manners, too*.*

Others seem to know it too - after all, there have been many imitators, soundalikes, and even some popular covers (don’t bother, it goes from sweet to saccharine very quickly), but no one has been able to hit the major points of a classic indie-pop song quite so well since.

Lee, I don’t know where you are these days. Most of us hear stories of you happily going to Everton home games and living a family life, although how the former can satisfy anyone, I’ve no idea. All I know is this - you searched for perfection when you had it all along. Let us hear what else you’ve got. It might even be, paradoxically, more perfect. You’ve more ways to release music *the way you want it heard* than ever. Bring it to us.

Then again, can you top your finest moment? The challenge is yours.
