**First of all, how are you today Paul?**

PH: Very well thanks.

**Was music present in your home during your childhood?**

PH: My Mum and Dad didn’t play anything. My Dad isn’t particularly musical. My Mum always loved music. The Beatles, Beach Boys. Stuff like River Deep Mountain High, Bad Moon Rising, Satisfaction by The Stones. She had all those singles. I’d say that was possibly my musical background. Classic stuff and great songs.

**As you get older and into your teens which bands or artist were influencing you?**

PH: The first album I got was probably The Rolling Stones. I remember buying it at Penny Lane Records. It was them supposedly recorded live at The Royal Albert Hall on one side and then singles on the other. But it wasn’t. It was from various gigs. I loved it. After that I probably got into the glitter stuff that kind of thing. T Rex’ Born To Boogie something like that.

**Was that a passing phase?**

PH: Yeah, I remember things being on the radio like Gene Genie, Rebel Rebel. I loved all that. It was before punk, so I got into a lot of rock stuff. My mates would play Led Zeppelin, but I only ever really liked the bluesy stuff. I saw ACDC at the Empire with the original singer which was just incredible. The power from a couple of chords you know rootsy stuff. I saw Motorhead and that just blew me away. I never liked Kiss or any of that Uriah Heep or Judas Priest. I thought that was nonsense. It was a strange time because you got into that, but my mates were into and I got into a lot of punk stuff. Road Runner by Jonathan Richman, Siouxsie and the Banshees, Television. You see Mike (Badger) was slightly older so Mike could go to Eric’s. I could only go to the matinees where I saw XTC and X-Ray Spex. A lot of it was about songs and those structured songs. I never liked the self-indulgent. I went to see the Led Zeppelin film and I thought it was self-indulgent crap. I really enjoyed the punk stuff. There was some great stuff. A lot of it is bluesy based.

**Captain Beefheart was massive for Mike and Lee. Was that an influence for you?**

PH: I knew of Beefheart but that came a bit later. I didn’t really get round to listening to him because people hadn’t really spoken about him. I suppose through The Stones probably 1974 I went to see Alexis Korner which was just incredible. In a pub in Liverpool. It was an eye opener. From punk stuff I went to college and you listen to other stuff. I had a conversation with Tommy (Scott) about this. You listen to post punk stuff. I loved The Only Ones and the first Bauhaus single which was great. The Bunnymen started too so I was listening to a lot of that.

**What prompted you to pick up a guitar and start playing?**

PH: I have a vague memory of this. I was born in 1963. I must have been four or five. I have a vague memory of seeing The Beatles on the TV. There is a picture of me with a beat-up classical guitar. That’s maybe where it came from.

**When did you seriously get into playing?**

PH: Probably teens

**Tell me about joining The La’s**

PH: It just happened to be right time, right place. Things like Son Of A Gun, Doledrum and Liberty Ship were already written. Probably Looking Glass had already been written as well. Other stuff like Timeless Melody was written in The Stables and finished in The Stables. I had those intro chords. Lee went “What’s that?” and Lee was always really good at clocking bits and pieces. I’ve got the original working out lyrics to that. I bet the guy from Pearl Jam would want to buy it! Probably for quite a lot of money! It’s got all the crossings out. I wrote loads of guitar bits for that song. I was really pleased that years later they were still in.

Way Out again that just sort of came about. I’ve got the original version without drums on. It’s slightly slower but exactly the same. I remember talking to Lee about it years later and he said “Yeah you wrote this bit Paul.” Knock Me Down was another written at the time.

There She Goes. I remember to this morning distinctly. Lee came in it was exactly like half ten/eleven o’clock. He goes “I’ve got this riff. It goes round these chords it’s like a circular riff.” He played the riff and probably the one other person who had heard it was his Mum cos he’d been playing it at home. He played it to John and I and I remember thinking “This is fucking amazing!” and the hairs on the back of my neck stood up. But I just thought, and I’ve always said this “Is anyone gonna hear it?” You do because you’re just in a group, I remember he played that riff and the chords ended on a D and I remember saying change it round to this other way of playing D sort of a C moved up because I thought it would sound a bit more melancholy and open. I wrote that other riff that went up. The reverse riff.

Over. That was near enough complete. I’ve got a list of songs and that was on the reserve song list. Over was one of those magic moments. We must have played it twice. I’ve got the original cassette. I’ve heard the other version that goes on at the end. We only ever played that song twice. It was summer and we were absolutely stoned out of our minds. We were all over the place, but it captured a magical moment. I’d bought this delay pedal and my idea was to get, but it didn’t work, a Golden Brown sound by The Stranglers. All these sorts of notes that float round. I think John wrote some of the lyrics to Over. My Mum had given me one of those ghetto blasters and I’d tape everything cos we’d forget things. It was taped on to a cassette tape on a little condenser that was built in. There were loads of ideas knocking around.

**I always think that the Over solo is really dreamy**

PH: It is because that’s exactly the sort of mood we were in. There are fluffs in there because we were just feeling our way around it. Lee kept those fluffs in years later! (laughs) I’m sure John and Jay have covered it.

**I saw them in Manchester, and they played it. I was made up.**

PH: Jay said to me did I do it well? I said, “You played it better than I did!” He did do a good job. At that time in our set list there were things like The Way We Came which was just a bit of a jam. Endless was finished around that time. I’m not bigging it up or anything but it was just a time and the chemistry was right. It was that moment were Lee had to do these songs. We used to finish them off in the day then you’d try them at night to see if they worked. I never really twigged this, but it was Martin Campbell from The Lightning Seeds who said to me “Do you realise that there were no new songs after that time?!” I think I Am The Key is half a song and I can’t really think of much else.

**You could say you were in the band at its peak and by that, I mean were they are still underground but nearing a record deal. Where there is still that rawness.**

PH: Lee would say himself that when the Go! Thing happened it fell to pieces. I could only cope with so much of going into the studio to record the same songs. None of it to me sounded right. We had John Porter from The Smiths. He put this weird sitar sound on a song, and it was like “What the fuck is that about?!” It might have been Doledrum, but I can’t remember. I think it was the wrong time to record a band like that. It was all recorded separately like Deacon Blue. Big sounds and gated drums. It wasn’t the time where you put a couple of microphones in front of you and said, “Let’s go.” That’s why I like the Pete Defreitas session. It’s covered in reverb but some of it is alright. It was recorded over two evenings. At that time, the set was started with Son OF A Gun and you would end with Looking Glass. I could play those songs now! They are in my head to this day.

**Were any other producers ever considered?**

PH: We did want Beefheart to produce the album. Our manager got in touch with Virgin and Virgin actually said, “We’ve got £30,000 for him if you actually find him let us know.” (laughs)

**How do you think that would have played out?**

PH: I think it would have been a disaster! (laughs)

**You were obviously with the band during legendary gigs like The Picket.**

PH: We always played The Picket for some reason. It was a nice venue to play. It was quite a big room. Bands used to play there a lot. I remember the night from the latest release (Upstairs At The Picket – Live & Dangerous 05.06.87) we played Failure twice and I only know that because in the second version Lee does his foot stamps and the reverb starts making a crashing sound and you can hear it. It’s great and sounds fantastic.

**How do eventually come to leave The La’s?**

PH: I got to the point where it was becoming incredibly disillusioning. Going in and out of studios playing the same songs with nothing really being released. I could sympathise with Lee that it wasn’t happening but there’s more to life than doing that. I could just see it going on forever. Which it did! (laughs)

**You seemed to see it for what it was quite quickly?**

PH: I was there for about a year. I always say The La’s was like the waltzer ride. It’s either so fast that you get thrown off or you see a little break and you step off! (laughs) I have no idea how John stuck with it for so long. I chatted to John in 1990 when There She Goes was a hit and he said, “I’m going to reap the benefits of this because I’ve worked hard and then I’m off” and that’s what he did. He saw that if he didn’t actually do something himself that was it because The La’s didn’t have much of a future.

**It’s amazing that John came into the band and could play bass and has gone on to become a prolific songwriter in his own right.**

PH: I like a lot of John’s stuff. Some of the tunes are amazing. He works hard at it. I was also really bowled over by John when he played John Lennon (Lennon The Musical). I only ever really talked to John about The La’s in recent times. I texted him around the time of The La’s stripped down. I remember saying The La’s were the only band that could play the songs but not as well and without a drummer or bass player! In the same order with no new songs! (laughs) Its just bizarre. If Lee had played in The La’s like that back, then he would have sacked himself. He doesn’t play in the same way and it’s like he’s forgotten. Lee used to play in an incredible rhythmical flamenco way especially in things like Son Of A Gun but he doesn’t anymore its vanished!

**Do you ever have any regrets about leaving?**

PH: No. None whatsoever. I think if you read the Matthew Macefield book it sums it up in there. It got to the point where I was never around. I wasn’t. I wasn’t around and I wasn’t bothered. I was there to do what I wanted to do. A lot of people got burnt by the band. There were days when I could see that it just wasn’t happening. Things just weren’t right. I though “How long is this going to go on for?” I know John and Chris Sharrock went off on holiday to Hawaii when they thought the album was finished and they came back to find Lee had scrapped the whole lot.

**People say that Lee was disgruntled that he wasn’t invited.**

PH: No that’s bollocks. Lee is one of these people. One day it will be amazing the next day its shit. Mike says the same thing where Lee would say to him “You’re the soul of the band” the next day “Haven’t you learnt anything?” Its hot and cold.

**It has got to be difficult to go along with**

PH: It’s all contradictions. I think he’s got so lost in the Lee world I don’t think there’s any escape. The record label cut off his money and tried to get him to do things. I think he earns a very nice living from There She Goes but then I have heard him say “What’s wrong with the other songs?” You can’t win! I know when Cast were big, and John had the big house he was really bitter. Hold on Lee if you had played the game and not disintegrated you could have had that!

**How big do you think they could have gone on to be?**

PH: They could have been pretty big. There She Goes was a hit in America. There’s something about being in a cult band though isn’t there? I have talked to Tommy about this because he wishes Space were a bit more of a cult band. Cult bands seem to have long lives. Mick Head will always have audiences. Edgar (Jones) to some extent.

**Mike got back involved with Lee in 1999. I know you were involved too.**

When we worked with Lee again in 1999 the mothership desk was set up and I knew how to work it. It was down about thirty or forty percent on one side. You could never release anything from it. Everyone portrays Lee as this Syd Barrett type character. He’s not. I went through every single reel to reel to see what could be used as in drum tracks, bass tracks whatever. You couldn’t use any of it. It was basically Lee jamming half an hour or sometime an hour singing the same phrase over and over again. The songs were half written. There was nothing there. What really hurt Mike in the book (In Search Of The La’s) was that Lee said the only intention that Mike had by getting back in touch was to do these albums. It wasn’t. It gives the impression that he worked with him for two weeks. We were there for eight months! He (Macefield) should have checked on that. Mike had kids and I’m paying rent for nothing. That’s why it all imploded again. Lee would go “The original demo is great.” You would put the original demo on the desk and Lee would say it wasn’t the original demo. Then eventually he would say that it was. It was just obstacle after obstacle. You can’t live like that. I saw Lee’s son who is lovely the other day. I said, “It’s ludicrous I could just come round with a desk or three microphones.” Not even that just him playing acoustic with new songs if they are finished off. He’d do really well.

**It would go crazy wouldn’t it. If he put anything out there would be so much interest.**

PH: Lee is just lost into his own little world. He’s got a studio set up in his shed, but I don’t know what he does with it.

**You and Mike obviously formed the Viper label together and have put together a number of La’s compilations and releases. One thing I have always liked about the Viper label is that it represents bands from under the radar like Tramp Attack and The Hokum Clones.**

PH: We haven’t done any new bands for ages because we can’t afford it and we haven’t found anything we like. I don’t think there’s anyone I want to record. Any label survives on their archive. We enjoy doing the archive thing and piecing it together. As music fans we want to find stuff that’s interesting and twist it in a surreal way. You don’t want to do the usual shite and make it more interesting. With The Hokum Clones there was a bit of a scene around them at the time and it was great fun doing it. They were a great band. Working with Howie (Payne) too was great. We recorded in my house but again some kind of magic happened with that. There’s not really a scene at the moment. The thing is though now the world is saturated with music and everything is free. You’re up against it. We won’t be doing any CDs just vinyl and downloads. Unfortunately, that’s just the way it goes. Half Man Half Biscuit do 20,000 – 30,000 units every time they sell. Shack would do the same. It keeps them afloat. Their back catalogue continually sells. We had Tramp Attack. It was around about the time The Coral were signed by Deltasonic. We loved Tramp Attack. We had them in the studio for the day and recorded five songs. Dave (McCabe) was like a kid in a candy shop. It was the first time he had been in the studio. He makes me laugh because he is out there. He arrived at the studio at 11 O’clock prompt and we said, “Where’s your guitar and amp?” and he was like “Shit I’ve forgotten them!” (laughs) He’s never forgotten us doing that. Dave is a great songwriter. It’s a shame because Tramp Attack just imploded like bands do. I’m glad we recorded them at the time when they were shit hot

**First of all, Mike how are you today?**

MB: I’m good. I’m always good and if I’m not it’s my own damn fault.

**So, we are in Conwy today. I was under the impression you were still in Liverpool. What brought you down here?**

MB: We just accidentally moved a few years back. We were out here visiting Mark McNulty who is a friend of ours. He’s a photographer. I asked him if there were any good antique places and he sent me this way and we saw this house and we fell in love with it. We bought it and moved here on spec. We had no plans of moving as we were happy in Liverpool. It was the right thing to do for us. The kids had just moved out and with what I do I can work anywhere. It’s been fantastic for us. I just walked over the mountain to get here. Most days I just walk then go home and create. I have a really great life you know.

**Are you back in Liverpool a lot?**

MB: I haven’t been back for a while actually. At one point I was going back once a week, then once a fortnight. Obviously, my folks are still there, and they come over to stay and I have some quality time with them here. The kids come over. I’m a grandparent now so if we were in Liverpool, they would pop in for a cup of tea but here they come and stay for a week. So, you actually get more time and it’s really nice.

**Are your children still based in Liverpool?**

MB: Yeah Amber is now a mum which is wonderful for us. She lives near Sefton Park. Ray lives in town and he’s a guitarist in a band called The Probes who are doing really well. They have an album coming out probably next year. They’ve done a number of singles. They’re great.

**What kind of style is it?**

MB: It’s kind of mid 70’s Neu. Can too but with a post punk thing. A bit Bunnymen, Joy Division but if you can mix all that up. It ain’t Rockabilly (laughs). It ain’t The La’s (laughs). He’s a great guitarist. He’s much better than I am. He’s a lead guitarist. Amber, our daughter, is an illustrator so that art and creative aspect is within them but if they had wanted to do anything else I would have been more than happy because it’s such a tough old game but it’s in them and it’s what they are. I’m so proud of them. They’ve never known any different. They’ve always been around music and art. It’s part of their DNA.

**Is the name The Probes inspired by the record shop Probe?**

MB: Well I don’t know. I was on the Probe record label and I’ve been friends with Geoff since I was a kid and going into the shop, so it probably had some kind of bearing on them (laughs).

**I was in Probe recently**

MB: It’s amazing it is still surviving, It’s the last man standing from that era. It’s a great shop. Ray’s band The Probes are managed by Peasey and Porcupine Management who manage The Bunnymen, Peter Hook and The Farm. They’ve been together for a really long time since they were young. They’ve done some great singles but it’s a tough old game. It was tough when I was doing it! It’s really tough now! (laughs) There’s so much music out there and everyone can record an album in their own bedroom.

**You really have to love music now as the opportunities to make money and a life out of it are dwindling with streaming. It just doesn’t pay anymore.**

MB: Well music is free now. We started the Viper label in 1999 the same year as Napster (laughs) so our record sales have gone steadily down. Downloads have gone steadily up. That’s just the nature of what it is but physical records have become a bit of a collector’s thing but Viper that’s another story.

**Is your wife artistic or creative?**

MB: Yeah, she is. She’s the one with the art degree. She’s got a degree in fashion and textiles. She used to make Eastern European triptychs which were goddesses and animals which hung on the wall. She’s getting back into her creativity, but she has been working for nearly ten or fifteen years with children with behavioural problems and puts her energy into that. She’s semi-retired now as well.

**So, growing up where did your creative side come from? Was that from your parents?**

MB: Quite possible. My sister is very creative. She had a knitwear company for years and is a designer. My mum was a secretary and my Dad was an electrician. It wasn’t really a thing to express yourself creatively in those days and when I was growing up it was like “it’s ok doing that as a hobby but what’s your job going to be?” Eve1ntually they realised I wasn’t capable of changing and getting a proper job (laughs). They kind of got it and I’m glad to say for a long time now they have been a massive support to me in what I’ve done creatively and followed what I’ve done with enthusiasm and genuine love. They listen to my CDs in the car and ask me about the lyrics. It’s wonderful. It wasn’t always like that! (laughs)

**Was there music in the house when you were young?**

MB: Well the music I remember was going on holiday and we’d have an eight-track cartridge in the car, and we’d be listening to Neil Diamond or Diana Ross. Ros is my older sister. I was lucky that she was into really good music. She had all the David Bowie albums, Neil Young, Shaved Fish, High Tide and Green Grass by The Rolling Stones. All these really good albums and that was my introduction plus all the seventies stuff which looking back now is fantastic. Singles you know? The first thing that really, really grabbed me was Dave Edmund’s I Hear You Knocking which is a wonderful piece of music that went to number one. I’ve always been a big Dave Edmunds fan you know and Nick Lowe who I met a few months back at a gig in Southport. He found out I was in The La’s. He was asking me about it and said “Were you in them when they supported Elvis Costello? Cos I saw them in Los Angeles and I don’t know how Elvis Costello went onstage after The La’s had been on” (laughs). I said I wasn’t and that was after my time, but I told him that we took Liverpool in the height of Thatcherism in the mid-eighties and he was like “What a story!” What a man Nick Lowe is. He’s a lovely man.

**So, growing up being into music and art. Was there one that was more important or were they intertwined?**

MB: They were always intertwined and then I kind of started writing words. I was very word driven and I loved playing with words. I was fascinated with them even before I was a visual artist. I liked music but I didn’t know if I had any designs on being a musician. It wasn’t as if I was a good musician as such, but I loved music. So eventually I had a real calling to start the guitar in my early twenties. I learnt a bunch of chords and started to write songs. The first song I wrote was “Trees and Plants” which I’m quite proud of. Thirty-five years later the environment is such a big issue and we were singing about it back then you know. Not in a hippy dippy kind of way in a punky way (laughs).

**When does Rockabilly come in for you?**

MB: Probably about 1983/1984. I’d just come back from London. I’d lived down there for about eighteen months and I started going to these Rockabilly nights. I’d been to some before I went to London and it was just raw unadulterated energy. The same kind of thing I felt when I went to Eric’s. It ticked all the boxes for me personally and when I heard Charlie Feathers and Johnny Burnette and the Rock n Roll Trio it was like game over. It was like punk rock, but they could play. They looked amazing. Recently I read in Mark Lewisohn’s book on The Beatles, Tune In. There is a whole chapter called Hi Ho Silver which is a lyric from Johnny Burnette and the Rock n Roll Trio. That was John Lennon’s favourite album and apparently, he used to skip tracks on all the other albums, but he couldn’t skip on theirs because they were the real shit. They were from Memphis. They were who Elvis called the scallies (laughs). They were boxers, they were tough fellas and they were not messing about. Check them out if you don’t know them.

**So, were there other bands before The La’s?**

MB: Yeah, I was in a band called The Kindergarden Paint Set. We were quite experimental. A lot of rhythm and percussion. It was three guys and three girls. We supported Mari Wilson. We were alright you know but just kind of starting off. Then some of them were students and had to get off and do that kind of stuff and it ended up amounting to nothing. So, I moved to London and I almost got a deal with Cherry Red Records. That didn’t amount to anything. It’s a shame really as it never got started but then again The La’s would never have happened cos I never would have come back to Liverpool because I came back to Liverpool specifically to form The La’s with Lee. But Lee didn’t even know. I just met him on a weekend back from London and he said he had been getting into Captain Beefheart. To be fair he was a rocker then. He wasn’t this kind of scally figurehead that he evolved into. He had the quiff, the leather jacket, and the biker boots. He was a cool and also an incredibly talented guitarist. He was hilarious, good fun and it was good times. I thought “I’m gonna go back and form a band called The La’s with Lee Mavers”. And that’s what happened.

**Lee had a band called Neuklon. Is that how you came to know him?**

MB: Yeah, they were like proper Huyton punks. We used to knock about together and just riot. Good days. Lee was playing bass in them and my mate Johnny Mellor who is the other grandad to our grandson. My daughter is with his son. He was playing percussion in Neuklon. He came round one day with Lee and that’s how I met him. I got them gigs supporting OMD in Eric’s just as they were starting. I put them on at our college do when I was at St Helens Art College in Eric’s. Rog Eagle saw them and said they were the future of rock and roll. They were just two synthesisers and a drum machine. They were raw and like Iggy Pop. They were really great. They expanded and Lee joined on bass. They kind of lost their immediate core focus though. Lee was always a talented bass player as well and before he was a guitarist.

**I must admit when I saw pictures of Neuklon I was surprised as they were almost new romantic.**

MB: Yeah well that was the time in 1981/1982. That’s the way it was. Loads of guys were wearing a bit of blusher. It was kind of a statement against scallies. The scallies at that time hadn’t got into pot they were still just fighters and drinkers, so we fucking hated them. They were just a load of footy thugs you know, and we saw ourselves as being more kind of cultured. Once the scallies started getting stoned, they all sort of melted (laughs) and that was when The La’s rode a crest of a wave because they all came on board. There was no us and them. There wasn’t the weirdos and the straights. We were all unemployed anyway and everyone was very politically savvy in Liverpool as we always have been. All the scallies got into Floyd and Roy Harper and then The La’s came along. All aboard!

**Beefheart was obviously massive for you and Lee?**

MB: Yeah, he was. I met him in 1981. My life is littered with these little moments of synchronicity. I love that. I was lucky enough to meet him and he had a profound effect on me. I became a big fan and I got Clearspot the first time round. When Lee came round with Johnny to our house in Huyton I asked him if he had heard Beefheart and he said, “I’ve heard me fart” (laughs) which was a typical Lee statement. The next time he came round he had got into him. He was like “I’m getting into Beefheart la.” I thought this guy is talented and cool enough for me to be in a band with if he wants to be in band with me. I kind of thought “I’m just gonna do it. He’s gonna be in a band if he likes it or not” (laughs). And it all worked out for a time. I’ve just written a piece about Beefheart and Viper have just done a new album called Magnetism II. I wrote the sleeve notes which is like an overview of Captain Beefheart and it’s something that I’m glad I’ve done. It kind of crystallised what he meant to me. When we first started, we were into all that spiky kind of guitars just letting it all come out, but I was also into Rockabilly which is a really curious kind of mixture (laughs). I suppose the closest thing would be the early Fall stuff you know which is very heavy on rhythm and just random stuff as you do when you’re young.

**It’s funny you mention The Fall as there are some early tracks where the vocals are almost spoken so a similar style to The Fall. I Did The Painting for example.**

MB: Yeah that comes from my word background really. That’s why when we released that stuff on Breakloose we kind of wanted people to know what it was like to start with and that we were kind of punky and it wasn’t all melody and sixties kind of spangly pop. It was all a bit kind of raw and dirty. If you listen back now it’s not that far away from hip hop. It’s Timmo playing his beats and me over the top with Lee doing spiky guitar riffs. There was melody with “Sweet 35” which was one of the early ones and then not longer after that it became more melodious

**Aside from Beefheart were there other key influences around that time?**

MB: He was the trigger really once I heard Lee was into him cos I knew that he was also a great rock and roll guitarist and then when we got together eventually I would bring in a song that was three quarters done and Lee would go “instead of playing a straight chord play this”. So, it would be based around the skeleton of what I had done, and we’d embellish and kind of finish it off with this lovely kind of veneer over it you know with his guitar playing. I’ve always used my guitar to write songs with chord progressions and I do little riffs, but Lee’s always liked the whole thing and he can do anything. We got a good body of songs together.

**It almost seems fated doesn’t it with The La’s and the things that have happened like the dream about the name.**

MB: It was really like nihilism. It’s reduced down to two letters. All the other bands at that time in the eighties had names that were so descriptive. They were all like sentence names like A Flock Of Seagulls, Orchestral Manoeuvres In The Dark, Echo and The Bunnymen. It was almost like paintings or pictures. Something like The La’s was like make up your own minds what it means. A lot of people assumed that it was the Liverpool “la” which I suppose is a side to it but the prime reason for it was that I saw the letters in a dream and I thought that’s an interesting name and would cause people to go “what’s that?” It was bringing a minimal element to it and letting people use their imaginations.

**I’ve read that when you returned from London you saw Lee outside the train station in the bus stop?**

MB: Yeah, my dad picked me up from the train station and he gave me a lift back to his house. We had since moved from Huyton to Prescott and as we’re going past there’s Lee in the bus stop. The first person I saw. I thought “There you go I’ll give him a call tomorrow” and I did, and he was like “Alright la yeah come over!” We started playing tunes in his sister’s bedroom.

**Was it instant?**

MB: I’d put lyrics down on the carpet in from of him and he’d start playing and I’d be like “I want it to be like this!” He’d do exactly what I had imagined it would sound like (laughs). It was a really natural chemistry and no effort at all. We did it for a few months and had a break. Lee said that around that time he was sitting off with a bunch of mates and getting stoned listening to Led Zeppelin and then he said “Where’s that tape I did with Badger?” and he said that he stuck it on and just thought “This is the fucking shit man! I’ve done nothing like this”. Even though it was raw, unfettered, and naïve it was real. It was honest. I always go for honesty in music before anything else. With singers for instance Celine Dion has a beautiful voice but I don’t own any of her albums you know. Whereas Neil Young you know (laughs). It’s where it’s coming from. That’s where it resonates with people you know. Musicianship doesn’t equate with quality.

**Sweet 35. How did that come about?**

MB: I think Lee’s guitar playing is off the scale and I don’t know if you know his grandad was a jazz guitarist and he kind of came up with that little riff. He started playing it and I did the lyrics over the top. I had these words. At that time Sweet 35 was about falling in love with a middle-aged woman and now it seems so young (laughs). It was all like sweet 16 and all these slogans saying you could only be sexy if you were young. We were a banner for the elderly (laughs).

**I remember reading a piece by Tommy Scott about Sweet 35.**

MB: Tommy was an original La’s fan and he used to come and see us at The Pen & Wig really early on. To be fair when we went our separate ways, Tommy stopped going to see The La’s because Tommy is like a little punk rocker and he loved the raw stuff. His favourite was Sweet 35 and Reindeer. He liked that era and when it became more melody and sixties, he didn’t really like it as much. Quite a few original fans did lose interest. You never hear about that and you never will because it was just a local thing and there was no media involved. When they got signed everything changed.

**When you listen to the tracks from that era it seems more experimental?**

MB: We were, and we were just starting. In many ways and when I look back it’s a pity that the way it was was the way it was. We were just getting started and we were just kids. It was such a shame as we had taken Liverpool. We’d done the job. Our cassette demo tape had reached Manchester. When I was in the band, they were getting circulated all around Liverpool and we had kind of done the hard bit. It was when that success came that unfortunately the shit happened. We should have been rolling in clover (laughs). Instead it all got a bit psycho. I’d put up with it to be fair for about six to eight weeks. It was ok up until about October 1986. Lee was becoming more volatile and downright provocative and it was almost like he didn’t want me in the band anymore. I was like “It’s not even your band mate”. John had been a big catalyst in a lot of people coming and John loved Lee. Just loved him. I didn’t get any allegiance from John to be fair even though I had got him in the band. I can’t say I wasn’t a bit disappointed in that. It’s only a natural thing but it was to be the way it was. Eventually I was like if you want it that bad you can have it. I’m not going to pretend that it was easy turning my back on two years of working in a band that I had started but I don’t take shit off anybody and so I did what I thought was right and it was the right thing to do because you shouldn’t take shit from anybody.

**I remember reading that he came to see you and his intention was to bring you back?**

MB: Yeah. It was a bit like once bitten twice shy and by that point I’d got something else going with the guys from the scheme. Danny Dean was on the scheme and was an amazing guitarist. I got a bass player Simon Cousins and we formed The Onset. We went back into country roots and rockabilly which is what I love. When we hooked up again in 1999, he told me that he was absolutely gutted that I wasn’t going to go back. But again, I think that that was the right decision. I’ve always been friendly with Lee, but it was the right thing to do because it would have been more of the same. I think as an artist anyone can tour, anybody can say I’m gonna do this and that. Anyone can have these fantastic high ideals like don’t compromise but if you don’t release anything and you don’t put an equals sign after the equation and offer it to the people what the hell’s it all about? As an artist you are sometimes wide of the mark and you don’t always get it right but you learn by it and you move on and do another album or another painting or a sculpture and you try and make it better. It’s that that qualifies you as an artist and the idea of me going back into a band where there was so much uncertainty, and all hinged on Lee’s volatile ideas its just like forget about it! I don’t care how talented you are. For a good while a lot of very loyal La’s fans loved Lee for that because he wouldn’t compromise and was like I’ve got my integrity. In the end you go you’ve got all this great music and great songs but you’re kind of coveting them for yourself when you’re not sharing them. When we sort of had our second renaissance in 1999 I think it was for about six months we saw each other most days and it was a bit of a love affair again trying to respark it and then eventually it was just the same old stuff. After six months I hadn’t got any work. I had wasted six months and had two kids. It was just like forget about it. He’s impossible in that respect.

**That’s pretty much the case now isn’t it. All these songs that we don’t get to hear.**

MB: That is against the universe. For me personally I couldn’t do it, but Lee is what he is. Around the time I released my first solo album which was reviewed in The Guardian and got quite a decent review. I got the paper one day from my Dad, open it up and there’s this whole page with a photograph of The La’s and underneath was this piece. It started off with “Although formed with Mike Badger his departure immediately made a difference for The La’s.” Almost like I had been holding them back. It was a real kick in the stomach. That’s exactly the opposite to what happened because when I left to be fair there was nobody there to go “Hey bollocks! What do you think you’re doing”? There was a leadership role which John didn’t have and Timmo didn’t have. If Lee and I hadn’t been there, then what band was there? Lee just became in control of things and with the greatest of respect he’s just no good at doing that. He thinks he is but he’s not. While we were together and on a 50/50 song writing basis and footing it was mutual and very productive. After that it just became madness.

**In the early stages are the lyrics largely yours?**

MB: No, the songs I sang were my lyrics. The only song that I wrote that Lee wrote the lyrics for was Callin’ All. There was a dispute about that which is another very hurtful thing because bands break up and musicians fall out, but the music is the music is the music. That’s sacred and that’s what it’s about. I saw it in Viz magazine that the album was going to be called Callin’ All I thought “It’s the first I’ve heard about it?!” I thought “Why has nobody mentioned it to me?” I basically wrote a letter to the record company and they sent a letter back via Rob Swerdlow managing them at the time saying “Lee says you have nothing to do with this song and he wrote it entirely by himself.” All I have to say to anyone who might dispute that is why isn’t it on the first album? It was going to be the title track. It was one of our best songs which we finished the set with. That was a low point I’ll tell you. I resented that terribly. Bands break up. People have different chemistries, but I don’t lose any sleep over it or anything because I’ve got a really good life and had a really good life since. I’ve got a bit with Feelin’ as well because Lee came up with that thing for my lyrics. I had put it down and explained the way I wanted it like the tempo and when it came to the guitar part followed by “My girl sits like a reindeer” it was just fun. It was a bit Half Man Half Biscuit. When that came out as the last single I was like “Thanks mate?!” I don’t ever make claims on things that aren’t my own because I have no need to and Lee is such a fantastic talent anyway why wouldn’t he just want to stick someone else’s name on it?

**Where does that come from? “My girl sits like a reindeer?”**

MB: My girlfriend who is now my wife used to sit with her legs to one side (laughs). It says it in the lyrics “with her legs to one side”. It’s a little bit salacious shall we say. Just fun. Young throw away madness!

**One song I really like from that period is Dovecot Dub**

MB: That’s Jasper’s song. We gave Jasper credit on the Breakloose album because he started it with a dubby bass line. We were all just in the studio just jamming about. It was lovely I was on the bongos and its got this break in it which we all fall back into. Jasper (Jim Fearon), he’s a lovely guy and he was the original bass player. It was 1984 and we went into the attic studio and did stuff like Dovecot Dub, Reindeer, Red Deer Stalk and Sweet 35. He was a great guy Jasper. I got to know him really well again when we got back together in 1999. He was around all the time then

**Another one I really like is I Did The Painting**

There’s a track on Tin Planet which I did the cover for by Space and Jamie from Space totally ripped off Painting and it’s called Piggies and he always goes “Sorry La!!” (laughs). He completely admits it! He was a massive La’s fan. His favourite La’s track is a track called Moonlight which again I was so happy with that song. I took it to Lee, and he brought in the stuttering kind of vocal. That was my song I wrote that, and I thought “Lee is gonna love this!” and the next thing it was the lip came out and he was like “Nah we are not doing that”. If that wasn’t good enough for The La’s I’m not good enough for The La’s because that’s all I am. It’s sad but it is what it is.

**When you leave and they continue with the name were you happy with that? It was your name after all**.

MB: I relinquished if. I jettisoned it. Unnecessary. Surplus to requirements. I didn’t have any problem with them carrying on as The La’s but for me it was never The La’s I was in and for Tommy Scott and the people who came to see us it wasn’t. It was like “Oh the *other* La’s”. Again, it is what it is. The whole story would have been completely different had it not been for There she Goes which is a beautiful perfect slice of pop. It would have been completely different for Lee as well because he wouldn’t have been able to have an income, bring up a family and just get stoned all the time. He would to have had to have done something (laughs). Hat’s off to him for writing it but he didn’t need to do anything else. He still doesn’t.

**Let’s talk about The Onset**

After I left The La’s I just got my head down and carried on writing. Got a new band together. Our album was out in 1988 on Probe and we went around Germany. At that time, I went back into my country rockabilly roots just as everyone else was dropping acid and getting into house music (laughs). It’s something I’m very proud of actually (laughs). It was hardly with the zeitgeist!

**So, did you come up with the name The Onset as well? It’s another great name.**

MB: Yeah, I did. It means the beginning or an attack. We almost scored a deal three or four times and we were getting airplay in Germany and France. People knew our songs over there more than they did in the UK. We were about to sign to Circa which was a subsidiary of Virgin in France and that morning that we were going to sign EMI took over Virgin! Our man got the boot and you just go “It wasn’t meant to be was it! (laughs)” Something was going to happen which wasn’t good which is the way I see it in these things. When you look back with the benefit of hindsight, I wouldn’t have changed anything because I think things happen for a reason and if that door had opened and another one had shut and so it goes. It’s part of being a creator and an artist you stand alone and it’s what you signed up for. You fight for everything. It’s why we are respected by other people. If you’re true to yourself good will out.

**I think it says a lot about you as a person. It didn’t work out with The La’s and you immediately went and did something else.**

MB: It’s like a shark you just keep on going or you die. Everything is moving in the universe and if you resist that and become static, you’re gonna pay the price. After years of music I woke up making a living as a sculpture. How did that happen? That is literally what happened.

**Was it a conscious decision to put music to one side?**

MB: Having a family and kids changed the dynamics of it. Before the kids came along you kind of had licence to be in a band. You could go away, and the Mrs wouldn’t mind you know. When you have kids and you’re just about covering costs things change. We were one of the first bands to play in East Berlin after the wall came down. Exactly thirty years ago. We went through Checkpoint Charlie and needed a visa to go through. It was an amazing experience that. What I could do and what I have always done is fill in sketchbooks, do collages and paint of an evening. I just accidentally started making tin sculptures. I had a knack for it and special awareness. My room started filing up with all these tin sculptures. No one else was doing that and still isn’t. I just ran with it and funnily enough the high point of doing my sculptures was when I got back in with Lee is 1999! (laughs). I was on Blue Peter and This Morning all these kinds of well-known shows. I was doing alright. Lee even came with me to Bristol Museum for my exhibition down there and we were knocking about all the time then. We were mates again. What happened in 1999 was we had all these plans to release the early demos which hadn’t been released and do a new album. We had enough songs between us to do a new album. I said the only way I will do it if it’s 50/50 like it was originally. He said, “I wouldn’t want it any other way” and then it was like “Learn all my songs. Learn all the guitar parts”. I did! (laughs) It got signed off by Lee and let me tell you that’s no mean achievement! But then it was like “When are you going to work on this new sing I’ve got?” but he was like “No you have to learn this one first”. It was becoming obsessive and I just thought “This isn’t working”. It was a shame because I had put six months into it. As I said before though as an artist you have to have a product otherwise, you’re not really an artist. You can talk about doing things or say I am conceptualising or there’s a reason like that’s not the best studio, tape, guitar, amp, time of day, the stars aren’t configured right or the weather’s wrong. How far do you want to go down that road to make excuses not to do something? In the end I thought I was wasting my time again. I had to get off but then I did have my art to go back to. We didn’t release The La’s demos until after Volume because I wouldn’t have done that album by me not allowing it to define who I was again, so I did my solo album.

**Are the tracks from Volume those which you were wanting to work with Lee on?**

MB: Yes, we did work on Silhouette. I have actually got a bunch of demos that people would kill to hear of me and Lee working together in 1999. He never ever lost his talent.

**Was he wanting to remake the debut again and then move onto another?**

MB: Well just releasing the demos would have been enough as he always wanted the album to sound more like the demos. Even me as a purist knew that no record label was going to release those four track demos. They had the vibe, but they wouldn’t do it because they wouldn’t get the airplay. In the 1980s especially that’s when it flipped over and became really high production. They weren’t going to release anything that was lo-fi. It was all gated reverbs and big Bon Jovi, U2 and Simple Minds kind of sounds. We hated all that shit! We were rock n roll.

**What were the new songs that were around at that time?**

MB: We were playing Minefields, Robber Man, Raindance and I was also learning stuff that I hadn’t played on like There She Goes which is an incredible thing to play. It was an incredibly nuanced guitar line. Lee would play it up and down with his index finger. No plectrum. A very interesting way to play and he nailed it. I was proud to do that. I was doing it for Lee and for me. It just didn’t amount to anything and you just go “This is a fucking nightmare. Call me a cab! (laughs)”

**Going back to your art and sculpture were there other artists that you were influenced by?**

MB: No I see it as being outsider art or punk art and that’s why I have never really been embraced by major galleries as they see it as being too quirky or a bit too real, heartfelt and human (laughs). I do a lot of work in schools and with special needs groups and that’s how I make a living. It’s DIY and quite punk rock in a way. Doing it on that level it’s very accessible to people making stuff from junk. It’s inclusive and not exclusive. A lot of art and particularly fine art is very exclusive with lofty ideals by the high minded. Its like the artistic world and corporate world. Like oil and water really. You shake it up and it’s going to separate. I don’t know which I am oil or water probably water! (laughs). Anyway, I have managed to make a living as an artist something that I’m really proud of. Anyone who can make a living from their creativity is successful. Whether you have had twelve million YouTube videos like There She Goes has or twelve it doesn’t matter. Anyone who equates success with talent is very misguided. Just put the radio on.

**Is your art a daily thing that you like to do?**

MB: It depends. I am always involved in something but it’s on my terms. I work very instinctively and intuitively. Its not always for my financial betterment (laughs). I could have done a lot of things that would have made me financially better off in my life but it just trust that things will work out for me. My grandad used to have a saying “Lucky in the big things” and I’ve always been lucky in the big things. My family and my relationship. I was always fortunate that I never got into pharmaceuticals and chemicals. You know I’ve smoked a load of weed and drank a lot of whisky over the years and that’s a big factor in a lot of people being dysfunctional.

**Have you ever done any sculptures that represent your time in The La’s?**

MB: No, I have done a lot of covers for other bands like Pete Wilkinson. I really love Pete. He’s a talented fella. We worked with him on Viper and I’ve done stuff with Tommy (Scott). He named the second Space album after my work. That was really great seeing stuff on billboards. A vindication you know. I’ve never had Art Council funding for anything. I’ve always done everything off my own back. I’ve always liked that because I’ve never had to compromise. My body of work is Mike Badger whether in music, art or in writing. At the end of the day that’s surely what it’s all about. I know that this is also what Lee thinks about his integrity. He genuinely didn’t like the first album because it wasn’t how he envisaged it. But get with the programme! (laughs). Deal with it and release your next album. That’s my idea of hell because he hasn’t really moved on. He had some momentum with The La’s after I left but really nothing for thirty years and its such a shame. I know everybody feels that. So much for the big hit record, fame, and notoriety. No thanks. With all respect just forget about it. It’s phenomenal how it’s never gone away with The La’s. They were really of their time. They did bring in that whole thing that happened with The Stone Roses and eventually Oasis. They kind of picked up the ball and ran with it. As with most things they happen in Liverpool first. That’s because of the nature of the beast. Liverpool is this incredibly cultured place going back centuries. It has a cosmopolitan nature. It’s not really part of England, Liverpool. It’s a Celtic city. It’s as much to do with Wales and Ireland probably more than England.

**How does the Viper label come about?**

MB: It just came about from having an album’s worth of material and Paul (Hemmings) said why don’t we release it? We thought we may as well do it ourselves. We did one album and learnt how to do that so we thought we may as well do another. Lee had helped us with them although he subsequently went on to say he knew nothing about it, and it was a case of surprise surprise thanks Lee! We thought we would release Breakloose and we went on and did a few other albums and we kind of gained a bit of a template. We thought all we have to do is get a distributor and we can do the artwork and mastering. We’ve never had much money to do promotion, but we knew there was enough interest in the cult aspect of what we were doing to make it viable.

**Did the arrival of the internet help with that?**

MB: It did a little bit. It’s been good and it’s been bad (laughs) because people were shifting real units as they call them in the 1990s and it all kind of finished.

**I am a massive fan of your first solo album Volume. Like all great albums it has a flow and the songs seem intertwined**

MB: That’s nice to hear. It was never really picked up on much. It got a few good reviews in the press, but some people didn’t get it. It’s a very honest album. The reason it’s low key, quiet and on an acoustic is because I was writing it whilst the kids were in bed! It was 1999 when the kids were little, so I wasn’t rocking out, but I was being a bit more introspective. Double Zero was a reaction to Volume. I had just done a completely acoustic album, so I decided to do an album all on electric guitar. A rock album you know. It got some good reviews but if nobody gets played on the radio nobody is going to buy it because nobody knows it exists. That’s always been the problem. The reason you don’t hear it on the radio is because it’s all sewn up in London and jobs for the boys. If I was down in London and going to the right parties and been an arse licker I’d be very well known. That wouldn’t be me though would it?

**Paul (Hemmings) plays on volume too?**

MB: Yeah, I play the hard Eko acoustic guitar which I love. I still play an Eros Italian guitar from the 1960s which is really heavy wood and great for recording. I played an Eko because it was in the studio and worked really good. So, I am playing the really hard steel string guitar and Paul’s playing the Spanish over the top. It’s got landscape hasn’t it? When we were recording it all the lights started going crazy on the mixing desk. It was so weird it started lighting up like a Christmas tree and the engineer was looking at the desk going “What’s happened?!” (laughs). It was like a phenomenon. After we recorded it, he said “Don’t think you’re recording it gain because that’s it! Those lights shouldn’t have been lighting up, but they were!”

**The thing I like about Viper is that it is cult and has bands on it that didn’t necessarily get the recognition or limelight.**

MB: People need to know that there’s more stuff out there. Most of the stuff on the radio is pretty mediocre, over produced shite. Not that I ever diss anyone in a band because I know how hard it is and respect anyone who makes music but the stuff that gets out there smells of money to me. You can smell the disinfectant on it. It’s inhuman. Charlie Feathers said, “You should be able to record the sweat of a man” and I tell you what that is what The La’s were about. That’s Lee. It was skiffle to be honest. It was kind of acoustic driven. That’s where rock n roll starts. Lovely little riffs and melodies.

**There are so many talented people who will just sell their soul to the devil.**

MB: More than ever unfortunately. People need to know that you’re a long time dead. The way I see all my artwork is that maybe we all strive for immortality but when you go from this place which is the only guarantee in this life all that’s left is what you did while you were here which is embroided into the life of others. You have to live with that. That’s the way I look at it. Great artists are rarely known in their lifetimes. If you’re really lucky you get the both. I feel fortunate that I was in bands in a time when there wasn’t really anything else. When I was in The La’s CDs hadn’t really come in and it was all tapes. We hadn’t gone digital and all you could spend your money on was albums, beer, and clothes. Everything has changed so dramatically over the past thirty years. I know I might only be a footnote in the rock encyclopaedia but at least I’m in it! (laughs)

**Have you found that since steaming has come in that has affected the Viper label?**

MB: We had a real hand to mouth existence with the Viper label, but it is something we have always done because we believe in it. Paul is brilliant with the managing of the label. We both contribute creatively to the way things are and the content of what goes out. We both come up with ideas. All that music wouldn’t be there if we had not had that tenacity and the belief to do it.

**Why was it named Viper?**

MB: We knew we were going to release the album Volume and we were waiting for a name and I was on holiday in the south of France in Nice and I was driving along and it my little girl was playing with a toy rubber snake and said “What kind of snake is this Daddy?” I was like “It’s a viper!” It came from Amber really. But it’s also a jazz term for weed and weed smokers.

**I remember record shopping in Liverpool at around 2000 and I picked up the Macefield book (In Search Of The La’s) and next to it was the Breakloose album. That’s where my fascination started.**

MB: That’s great to hear. It’s vindication of why we do it. Everybody just thinks that The La’s is the first album. I do stand by the bravery of releasing Breakloose because it was such a well know band and it was nothing like the album. Some people think it’s shite and others go wow!

**The track Breakloose is like a punch in the face.**

MB: That was a one chord song in E, and I wanted it just to be relentless. A fist of defiance. To get out of the rut of Thatcherism. Breakloose! Let’s have a revolution! I still feel exactly the same. More than ever. God if there was ever a time for that song. Lee brought in the descending riff in the second verse. It goes down “I shook the shackles, kicked the ball. Found myself and found us all”. We used to open with that song. It was like kicking the doors down. That’s when we were on fire. That would start it, and Callin’ All would finish it. It was of its time. It was short lived but I’m glad we did it. I would have liked the album the way we played it live in 1986. It would have started with Breakloose then Doledrum, What Do You Do? Sweet 35, Son Of A Gun, Reindeer, I Can’t Sleep. Eventually Viper went on to release the second album Callin’ All which was the original demos of the first album. In 1987 Paul was with them and did Timeless Melody and There She Goes. Something that surprised me was that Paul told me that Timeless Melody came off the back of Open Your Heart. I love that song its one of my favourite La’s songs after I left but the chord progression into the chorus is the same. It goes “Open Your Mind” at the end. It’s nice to know I had a little part of that, but I wouldn’t make a claim on the credit like I would some of the other songs. But with Breakloose I used to listen to a lot of reggae music and rebel music. The Clash too. That’s a big part of where I was coming from. Lee had also seen The Clash in Eric’s. Mick Jones had got them in, but he used to let all the kids in through the back door. They were lovely inclusive people. As it should be. In Open Your Heart it’s a very lovely gentle song but it does have that intensity about it around taking control for ourselves otherwise we are just gonna get rolled over. I don’t think there will be a utopian watershed moment where everything is fixed because that’s not what life’s about, but it can certainly get better. It will get a whole lot better when this shitty government are kicked into touch.

**Have you ever gone into other forms of art such as acting?**

MB: I would really like to get into acting. It’s a secret passion. I’ve got a treatment for a film which I’ve written. A horror film which I want to set round here. We were going to go into production with it in winter but my friend who was going to work on it with me died. I’m really sad about it. It’s something that I would like to do. You know your own strengths. Working on the instinctive and intuitive part of who you are is something I have always done. I have someone else who might be able to help me, and I’ve done a few scripts actually. I don’t believe in pushing something if it’s not naturally happening. In my life things have happened when they have supposed to. There’s no point throwing energy at something when the universe says not to.

**You wrote a book “The Rhythm & The Tide”. What inspired you to do that?**

MB: I was so glad to have done that. It allowed me to draw a line under that part of my life. So much has been said about The La’s over the years and when it’s said enough it becomes currency and the norm. One thing I’m glad it says in the book is that There She Goes is not about heroin. The band that I started is associated with heroin. I hate that. There’s nothing nice about heroin it’s a pile of shit. It’s there for people who are in pain. It’s the earth giving you something if you are in pain. If you take it and you haven’t got pain, you soon will have. I confirmed it with Lee. I asked him even though he did get into it and it’s been a big part of his life. Thankfully, I’ve never got into pharmaceuticals. The Rhythm & The Tide is the toing and froing of life. The pulls and pushes that you have. The music, living by the sea and the Mersey. The history of Liverpool. Liverpool University Press wanted me to write a book about The La’s and I just said no it’s two years of my life. Tim Peacock who I wrote it with is a lovely guy. Without him I wouldn’t have been able to corral all the information and submit it in the way we did. It took about three or four years. When we submitted it to Liverpool University Press, they got it. They knew why I had said I’m not going to do a book on just The La’s. It’s a much bigger story like playing in Berlin in 1989 just after the wall came down. That’s interesting. Not sitting round and getting smashed in a rehearsal room not doing anything. It allowed me to draw a line under a lot of stuff to do with myself and The La’s. There had been some stuff done in another book which I never read. I did an interview with the guy that wrote that book, Matthew Macefield. Eventually he got to speak to Lee, and he contradicted a lot of what I said, and Matthew never came back to me to clarify. It quite upset me that Lee said he knew nothing about the demos coming out when he was part of that process. He said that he received no money for it when he was on all the publishing. It didn’t make that much but people have benefited from that label by being able to put music out which otherwise wouldn’t have happened. We have never been a big label.

**I revisited the Macefield book recently and I must admit I feel the chapter on you is a little brief given the fact that you started the band and your contribution**.

MB: As I say I haven’t read the book, but The Stairs did a reform gig at The Academy and it was fantastic. I love The Stairs. Somebody came up to me after my book had been out about a week and said why were you so nice about Lee in your book? I didn’t realise that I had been that nice. I prefer the smell of roses. I’d rather accentuate the positive things in life that we have been a part of rather than the negative. Let’s talk about the good things.

**Macefield states in his book that he got the impression that you might not want to speak about The La’s in that much detail again.**

MB: It is what it is The La’s. I don’t tell anybody that I was in The La’s. In the summer we had a barbeque with people from the village and a lady approached me and said “Mike, we *know* who you are” (laughs) But I like the fact that I don’t let it define me. I’d much rather it be like that.

**The Rhythm And The Tide cover picture is fantastic.**

MB: I’d just got a new guitar and Lee was showing me a few chords. We are getting there. Lee has got the boots on that I gave him. Punky monkey boots. It’s at the back of my flat on Falkner Street. About 1985, I think. Happy days. They were great. I have such fond memories and Lee does as well. It’s a very iconic picture. I’m so happy that that moment was captured because there weren’t many cameras around (laughs).

**One part of the book that interested me was that you would get called upon by The Lightning Seeds to be involved in certain things.**

MB: I was friends with Ian (Broudie). Our kids were young at the same time and although he was The Lightning Seeds he was trading as a band and he needed a guitarist in videos. I’m not gonna turn down £200 when my Mrs is pregnant (laughs). I don’t tell anyone that I was in The Lightning Seeds because I didn’t gig with them. I did some TV with them. Morning kids’ TV miming. The one I love is Lucky You. That song follows us around. Whenever I hear that song, I think something good is going to happen and it always does. Its magical. I think there might have been a door open for me to join the band because I know Ian was after a guitarist. But I wasn’t going to be in Ian’s band because I’m a singer-songwriter. At that time Paul (Hemmings) lived opposite Ian and wasn’t doing anything. The next thing Paul’s on the world tour the little bastard (laughs). One thing that disappointed me about my book was that Mojo, Q and NME never reviewed it. Its such an important story. The only actual review it got was in Record Collector and that’s because Tim Peacock is one of the main writers for Record Collector. You know your music doesn’t get played on the radio, your books don’t get reviewed and you think why bother? You bother because you’re an artist and as I said before all you have left is what you have created. I’ve been over to America four of five times in the past ten years playing to people over there. I’ve been living it you know. Live for a living. But yeah, the book gave me the opportunity to write about our second honeymoon in 1999. I didn’t want people to think that I had any animosity towards Lee, or I hold on to any bitterness about The La’s because I don’t. I’m far too busy getting on with life and enjoying it. I do genuinely really want to help Lee get out of this corner he painted himself in. I committed six months you know. I didn’t give it a few days or weeks I went in with both feet and we had some really good times. In the end it was like “I’ve got a life Lee and I’ve got a family to feed”. He did give me shit saying, “You’re only been in it for yourself” and it was a case of forget it.

**Have you any plans for more solo albums?**

MB: I’ve got an album of collaborations that I want to release on my own label Generator which I’ve done a few albums on including my solo stuff. I’ve done a few albums on Viper as well but its better if I release that stuff on my own because if it doesn’t recoup immediately, I’m only answerable to myself. I’ve just recorded a video for a new single called “Beat The Path to Your Door” which is probably the most hardcore energetic thing I’ve done in my life! Mark McNulty has just shot the video on all the trails and tracks up on the mountain. That’s gonna come out soon on a New York label. I’m gonna release another single to follow it up called “Shoot Me In The Head (If I Ever Become One Of Them)” which is about the current political climate (laughs). I’m recording with Ray my son and he plays lead guitar.

**Are there any new bands I should be listening to at the moment?**

MB: I’m probably the wrong person to ask! Probes. The Floormen too. Their psychedelic. They are mates of Probes. I’m out of all that though. My problem with Liverpool is everyone is stuck in the sixties. They should be stuck in the fifties like me! (laughs)

**And finally, you’re a blue. What are your thoughts at the moment?**

MB: (Laughs) Ah same old same old. Welcome to my life! Every year they offer you hope and kick you in the balls! I wouldn’t have it any other way either! Its in your DNA. You are what you are.

First of all Barry, how are you?

BS: Great thanks

What are your earliest memories of music?

BS: Musically, the first time I ever felt moved was by things like the Planet Suite by Holst, Public Image by Public Image when I was 12, things like the Everly Brothers Little Richard, Surfin’ USA by The Beach Boys.

When did you start playing guitar?

BS: The first time I picked up a musical instrument was when I was around 13 years old, I used to go to a friend of mine who had a guitar which I became curious about. Like a lot of people of that age, I had no real other interest in anything (apart from science fiction).

I started writing stupid songs immediately on the guitar, I was in school and had a band called the Western Diplomats and at the time I was into things like Cabaret Voltaire, The Fall, Echo & The Bunnymen and I just wanted to play fucked up music to play with peoples’ heads. I rediscovered that attitude…

Drugs were a big influence on me as a musician, as they usually are when you start playing as a teenager getting high. The big step for me was taking LSD and listening to things like Piper at the Gates of Dawn. Probably not a good view to hold in Liverpool, but I believe every other Pink Floyd record is reprehensible nonsense.

You were in bands before The La’s like Marshmallow Overcoat?

BS: By the time Marshmallow Overcoat knocked around in 1983, the psychedelic experience was influencing and informing the way I heard and created music. If you know you know, the rest of you at the back can I ask questions at the end.

What were your memories of The La’s before joining the band?

BS: The La’s is Lee Mavers and three other people. When The La’s started, they were very ramshackle and Mike Badger had songs that took him in a different direction to Lee. When Mike left, it became Lee’s band which is absolutely correct. We used to hang out together and had mutual appreciation for each other’s band, it’s fair to say that The La’s were fans of Marshmallow Overcoat.

What is your favourite track by The La’s?

BS: My favourite La’s song is Raindance.

You were in the band when the legendary “Kitchen Tapes” bootleg was made. What was that like to be part of?

BS: The kitchen tapes was just a moment in time, we rehearsed a lot and it showed in terms of how tight we were.

Having been in the band from 1988-1989, what was your opinion of the Go! Discs album release?

BS: Although there have been a lot of divergent views on the released version of The La’s album, I think it’s extremely clear that Steve Lillywhite was absolutely the wrong producer for that band. He produced stadium rock bands like Simple Minds and U2. To produce an organic Scouse band playing space shanties was not in his skill set.

After leaving The La’s you played in a number of great bands.

BS: I have been in lots of bands, Walkingseeds was a band the Kurt Cobain liked but look what happened to him! There are too many bands to mention that I played for, Cast? Thankfully, I re-discovered the joy of fucking with peoples heads.
