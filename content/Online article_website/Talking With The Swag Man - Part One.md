### The Huyton Punks

*Seen it, done it, got the T-shirt.*

If you wear the name of your favourite band across your chest, chances are it was put there by Johnny Mellor.

From post-punk 80’s Liverpool to the digital age of global mega-gigs, Johnny toured the world with some of the biggest and most influential names in music, including Stevie Wonder, Ice-T, Noel Gallagher and ZZ Top.

Wether facing down drug mutiny on a hip-hop tour bus or trading punches with an international footballer, Talking With The Swag Man is a hilarious Boys own adventure of an ex Sex God turned international merchandiser/smuggler/nightclub owner who lived to tell the tales.

I spent a few evenings with Johnny talking about his adventures on the road. I will serialise them here on Substack.

The story starts in Huyton, Liverpool …

*Our kid was in the Boys Brigade. That’s where it all started, really. Why he joined up, I have no idea. I think it was cos we weren’t church goers and it was something to do on a Sunday. He had a bugle and it drove our arl fella barmy. Me Dad wasn’t much of a music lover - he was into barbershop quartets. I wasn’t arsed about it, but there were other lads in the BB that had side drums and one of them ended up in our house. I used to rattle round on it - I’d have been about six or seven at the time.*

*I used to watch Top of The Pops when I was a kid, and I always thought it was a girl’s programme. I never went into school and said, did you see Top of the Pops? Everyone would be talking about The Six Million Dollar Man or The High Chaparrall. I never mentioned the fact that I liked Top of the Pops or any kind of music. It was kinda like my guilty secret. There was a fella up the road who was the drummer in the social club and he had a drum kit and a perm and I just thought he was amazing. He played in a little threesome, they were the backing band in the local working mans club. To me, it was like having a mega star living at the end of the street. So I kinda got into drums. A mate of mine, Andy Mac, he had a guitar and we used to form little bands. I didn’t have a drum kit. Me ma had a camel’s saddle - God’s knows where from - so I used to play that.*

*The first band I ever properly got into was T Rex. Our kid was listening to Deep Purple and Yes, and whatever he was into, I went the opposite way. My first single was either Ride A White Swan or Hot Love. This would have been the very early 70’s. I was mad for all that glam stuff - T Rex, Bowie, Roxy Music. And then punk happened, and the shock of the pistols. It was like a hand grenade going off. I left school at the age of 16 in 1977 - perfect timing. I was oblivious to all that London thing in ’76. Punk in Liverpool was wearing your Dad’s raincoat and drainpipe kegs and a little thin black tie.*

*There was a club in town called The Swinging Apple, which was kinda like a club for skinheads. A bit more alternative than your average chicken in a basket gaff. Eric’s was up and running by then too, but The Apple was a lot moodier. The main reason I went down wasn’t anything to do with music, though. The Echo had done this Punk expose feature thing and there was a photo of a girl with a kettle for a handbag and fishnet tights on and she’d cut the arse out of her tights, showing all her bare arse. Me and my mate Timmo were like wow, girls with bare arses! We’re going there!*

*I’d been telling everyone I was a drummer. I could hold down a basic beat on the camel’s saddle, so in my head I was a drummer. The next thing to do was get into a proper band. My mate Mike Badger, who I’d known since I was six, said he was managing a band called Neuklon, who were a kind of early synth band. Timmo was more into motorbikes, so he wasn’t arsed. The line-up at that time was me on drums, Lee Mavers on low-slung bass, which he played JJ Burnel style, a lad called Robbie - who never ever turned up for rehearsals but who would turn up for gigs - and a madman of a singer called Lloyd. Lloyd had an American girlfriend called Pepsi who played a bit of keyboards. He also had long badly dyed orange hair and was skinny and weird looking but he was also a really good boxer. His arl fella was fucking heartbroken that he was knocking about with us We used to go round his house and his arl fella would just sit there scowling at us, cos Lloyd had given up boxing to dye his hair and become a punk. We lived in Huyton, this council estate with nothing going on, but our band become the infamous Huyton Punks. People round the estate would be like, ‘ey you set of knobheads! Me and Mavers weren’t shy, we were proper scallies who would happily gob off back, but Lloyd could knock people clean out. So, for better or worse, every fucker knew who we were.*

*Ubeknownst to us, people in town knew who were too. I found out recently that Bill Drummond had said “Neuklon are the future of music”. Obviously he was wrong, cos it’s the future now and Neuklon are still in the past. We had a good following though, and did loads of storming gigs. It all ended in tears when Lee fell out with Lloyd and the drum kit got sold. There was some big shouting match I can’t even remember what it was about. Anyway, Mavers went stage left, swiftly followed by my good self.*

*Lee’s capacity for laziness was astounding. I’d leave work, go home and have me tea then go round to the Mavers’ household. His Ma would be like, oh alright Johnny, just go upstairs, he’s in bed. This would be, like, half past five in the afternoon. But when he did manage to drag himself down the stairs, he was starting to write songs. Good ones. Proper ones. The plan was that me and him would get a band of our own together, but it never quite happened. The nearest we got was going busking in St Helens. Lee had a battered old acoustic and I had a pair of bongos. We had a repertoire of two songs - “Rockaway Beach” by The Ramones and “Golden Brown” by The Stranglers. We’d just play them two tunes over and over again. At the end of the day we’d have amassed about seventeen quid in pennies and argue all the way home over who’d get the odd leftover penny on a fifty-fifty split. Neither of us would budge an inch. It wasn’t the principle, it was the money. Both of us were skint.*

*I started knocking about around town and Lee stayed in Huyton with Mike Badger and formed The La’s. One of the places I frequented was the Everyman Bistro, along with this girl called Janet Gray. She took me to all the in places where the trendies hung out. At that time in Liverpool, the city centre was buzzing with bands, most of them with - or about to land - record deals. Some of Jane’s mates had formed a band called The Pale Fountains. They were a lovely bunch of lads who loved football as much as music. We spent most of our time kicking a ball about. As soon as I finished me apprenticeship I moved into a flat down Rodney Street with Mick Head. There was a massive buzz about the Pale Fountains and I was on the periphery of it. They were just so different to anything else around at the time. I started getting out of Liverpool a little bit - playing the bongos, driving the van, humping gear about. Then The Palies got signed by Virgin Records and were swept off with boatloads of cash and Geoff Love Orchestras and I was sorta left behind.*

*This Welsh kid called Alan moved into the upstairs flat at Rodney Street. He’d come to Liverpool and joined a band called the Wild Swans. Me and Alan really hit it off, and through him I met Andy Eastwood. And that’s when I started my career as a smuggler …*
